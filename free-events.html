    OK, so that was the concert. The other event takes a little explanation and I hope I will explain it well. First, it was a debate hosted by the debate club. The tradition of debates is not very strong in Israel but it is very common it England and North America (and maybe other places). Usually debates have a political nature but not always. The debate this time was about something which happened in November last year in Canadian politics. The system here is kind of similar to Israel but a little different. To make it easier, they have a position here called the "Governor-General" (GG), which is not an elected position and is officially the representative of the Queen of England in Canada. It is a little bit like the Israeli president in that it is usually taken as a ceremonial position but it has some real powers that are needed in times of political uncertainty.

    The comparison with Israel works very well because we had a similar situation with the last election. Livni officially won more votes then Bibi and Peres, as the president needs to decide who getsthe first chance at forming a government. This is not easy, you don't just choose Livni because she won one more seat. The GG in Canada has similar responsibility (among others) and there is a political process called "prorogation" in which the parliament (the KNESET) is suspended for a few weeks and when they come back they start with a new agenda, meaning, all the different laws they were working on start from fresh. Never mind why they do it, take it as a fact, and the GG is the one who decides to do it when asked by the Prime minister. When the Prime Minister asks the GG to do it is usually ok since the Prime Minister has a majority support in the parliament

    A few weeks after the last Canadian elections the biggest party (the Conseratives) didn't have a majority and the other three parties wanted to try a coalition. In Israel every government is a coalition but in Canada it is not common. The Prime Minister, who was just elected and haven't actually faced a vote of confidence, went to the GG and asked her to proroge (suspend) parliament. This was an awekward situation since usually the GG acts based on what the Prime Minister asks because he has the confidence of the parliament, but this time it wasn't clear - we just had an election and there was not a vote of confidence yet (or a budget vote, which is usually taken as a confidence vote) and it was clear that if we had a vote he would lose. He was, in fact, trying to avoid the vote of confidence because he knew he will lose.

    So this was a big deal here. The GG ended up doing it and we didn't have parliament for about 7 weeks. Anyways, the reason I'm telling you all of this (other than that I find it interesting) is that the debate today was about whether or not she did right. They had some very interesting people in the crowed including the most famous professor in Canada for constitutional law and a previous Governor-General.
